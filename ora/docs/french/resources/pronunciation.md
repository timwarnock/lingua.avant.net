# <span tooltip="Pronunciation">Prononciation</span>

French descends directly from the Vulgar Latin spoken in Roman Gaul. After the collapse of the Western Empire, this spoken Latin developed over centuries into the language we call French. The oldest surviving text in a form of French is the Serment de Strasbourg of 842. Old French appears in the great medieval epics, and by the time of the Ordinance of Villers-Cotterêts in 1539 French had replaced Latin as the language of law and royal administration in France.

While the official liturgy of the Church remained in Latin for most of its history, the Rosary has always been a prayer of the people. In French cathedrals, villages, and later in Quebec and across the missions, the faithful recited "<span tooltip="Our Father">Notre Père</span>" and "<span tooltip="Hail Mary">Je vous salue, Marie</span>" in their own tongue. The Académie française, founded in 1635, worked to fix spelling and pronunciation standards that still guide the clear, traditional delivery of these prayers today. The result is a measured, flowing style perfectly suited to the steady repetition of the Rosary.

Pronunciation of French is often left to natural exposure and practice -- a slow process of discovery. English speakers commonly guess from spelling, import English glides, miss the nasal vowels, and skip liaison. Effective teaching short-circuits that with clear rules and examples.

!!! quote "Henry Widdowson"
    The whole point of language pedagogy is that it is a way of short-circuiting the slow process of natural discovery and can make arrangements for learning to happen more easily and more efficiently than it does in natural surroundings.

## Phonemes

French has *11 oral vowels*, the fixed sequence <span onclick="new Audio('../phonemes/oi.mp3').play()" style="cursor:pointer;"><strong>oi</strong></span> (as "wa"), and *4 nasal vowels*. Oral vowels use the mouth only; nasal vowels use mouth and nose together.

French vowels are pure: each holds one quality from start to finish. English often glides (the o in "go", the a in "name"). Hold French vowels steady.

!!! success "Oral vowels"
    + <span onclick="new Audio('../phonemes/a.mp3').play()" style="cursor:pointer;"><strong>a</strong></span> like a in "father" -- e.g. <span onclick="new Audio('../phonemes/grace.mp3').play()" style="cursor:pointer;"><strong><span tooltip="grace">grâce</span></strong></span>
    + <span onclick="new Audio('../phonemes/e-closed.mp3').play()" style="cursor:pointer;"><strong>é</strong></span> closed; "ay" without an English glide -- e.g. <span onclick="new Audio('../phonemes/beni.mp3').play()" style="cursor:pointer;"><strong><span tooltip="blessed">béni</span></strong></span>, <span onclick="new Audio('../phonemes/sanctifie.mp3').play()" style="cursor:pointer;"><strong><span tooltip="sanctified">sanctifié</span></strong></span>

+ <span onclick="new Audio('../phonemes/a-grave.mp3').play()" style="cursor:pointer;"><strong>à</strong></span>, <span onclick="new Audio('../phonemes/u.mp3').play()" style="cursor:pointer;"><strong>ù</strong></span> distinguish words (<span onclick="new Audio('../phonemes/a-grave.mp3').play()" style="cursor:pointer;"><strong>à</strong></span> = "to") without changing the vowel from plain <span onclick="new Audio('../phonemes/a.mp3').play()" style="cursor:pointer;"><strong>a</strong></span> / <span onclick="new Audio('../phonemes/u.mp3').play()" style="cursor:pointer;"><strong>u</strong></span>

!!! success "Nasal vowels"
    Four sounds. Air flows through nose and mouth. After these vowels, written n or m usually only marks nasality -- not a second consonant -- unless a following pronounced vowel brings the consonant back (e.g. <span onclick="new Audio('../phonemes/pleine.mp3').play()" style="cursor:pointer;"><strong>pleine</strong></span> de).

    + <span onclick="new Audio('../phonemes/an.mp3').play()" style="cursor:pointer;"><strong>an</strong></span> (also en, am, em) -- e.g. <span onclick="new Audio('../phonemes/saint.mp3').play()" style="cursor:pointer;"><strong><span tooltip="holy">saint</span></strong></span>
    + <span onclick="new Audio('../phonemes/in.mp3').play()" style="cursor:pointer;"><strong>in</strong></span> (also im, ain, ein, aim) -- e.g. <span onclick="new Audio('../phonemes/pleine.mp3').play()" style="cursor:pointer;"><strong><span tooltip="full">pleine</span></strong></span>
    + <span onclick="new Audio('../phonemes/on.mp3').play()" style="cursor:pointer;"><strong>on</strong></span> (also om) -- e.g. <span onclick="new Audio('../phonemes/nom.mp3').play()" style="cursor:pointer;"><strong><span tooltip="name">nom</span></strong></span>
    + <span onclick="new Audio('../phonemes/un.mp3').play()" style="cursor:pointer;"><strong>un</strong></span> (also um)

    Many speakers in northern France merge <span onclick="new Audio('../phonemes/un.mp3').play()" style="cursor:pointer;"><strong>un</strong></span> toward <span onclick="new Audio('../phonemes/in.mp3').play()" style="cursor:pointer;"><strong>in</strong></span>. Either way, leave the vowel nasal without a hard final n.

French has *17 consonant phonemes* (p b t d k g f v s z ʃ ʒ m n ɲ l ʁ), plus *3 glides*: y as in <span onclick="new Audio('../phonemes/fille.mp3').play()" style="cursor:pointer;"><strong>fille</strong></span>, w as in <span onclick="new Audio('../phonemes/oui.mp3').play()" style="cursor:pointer;"><strong>oui</strong></span>, and the rounded glide in <span onclick="new Audio('../phonemes/huile.mp3').play()" style="cursor:pointer;"><strong>huile</strong></span>. Most consonants are close to English. French p, t, k have less aspiration than English. Pronounced stops are fully released.

!!! success "Consonants"
    + <span onclick="new Audio('../phonemes/r.mp3').play()" style="cursor:pointer;"><strong>r</strong></span> uvular -- light friction at the back of the throat, not an English or Spanish roll -- e.g. <span onclick="new Audio('../phonemes/pere.mp3').play()" style="cursor:pointer;"><strong><span tooltip="Father">Père</span></strong></span>
    + <span onclick="new Audio('../phonemes/j.mp3').play()" style="cursor:pointer;"><strong>j</strong></span> = "zh" as in "pleasure" -- e.g. <span onclick="new Audio('../phonemes/jesus.mp3').play()" style="cursor:pointer;"><strong><span tooltip="Jesus">Jésus</span></strong></span>
    + <span onclick="new Audio('../phonemes/ch.mp3').play()" style="cursor:pointer;"><strong>ch</strong></span> = "sh"
    + <span onclick="new Audio('../phonemes/gn.mp3').play()" style="cursor:pointer;"><strong>gn</strong></span> = "ny" as in "canyon" -- e.g. <span onclick="new Audio('../phonemes/regne.mp3').play()" style="cursor:pointer;"><strong><span tooltip="kingdom">règne</span></strong></span>, <span onclick="new Audio('../phonemes/seigneur.mp3').play()" style="cursor:pointer;"><strong><span tooltip="Lord">Seigneur</span></strong></span>
    + <span onclick="new Audio('../phonemes/qu.mp3').play()" style="cursor:pointer;"><strong>qu</strong></span> = "k" -- e.g. <span onclick="new Audio('../phonemes/qui.mp3').play()" style="cursor:pointer;"><strong>qui</strong></span>
    + *c* before e, i, y = <span onclick="new Audio('../phonemes/s.mp3').play()" style="cursor:pointer;"><strong>s</strong></span> -- e.g. <span onclick="new Audio('../phonemes/cieux.mp3').play()" style="cursor:pointer;"><strong><span tooltip="heavens">cieux</span></strong></span>; otherwise "k" -- e.g. <span onclick="new Audio('../phonemes/croix.mp3').play()" style="cursor:pointer;"><strong><span tooltip="cross">croix</span></strong></span>
    + *g* before e, i, y = "zh" (same as <span onclick="new Audio('../phonemes/j.mp3').play()" style="cursor:pointer;"><strong>j</strong></span>); otherwise hard g -- e.g. <span onclick="new Audio('../phonemes/grace.mp3').play()" style="cursor:pointer;"><strong><span tooltip="grace">grâce</span></strong></span>
    + *h* is never sounded -- e.g. <span onclick="new Audio('../phonemes/homme.mp3').play()" style="cursor:pointer;"><strong>homme</strong></span> (the written h is silent; you hear "omm")
    + final consonants are usually silent -- <span onclick="new Audio('../phonemes/vous.mp3').play()" style="cursor:pointer;"><strong>vous</strong></span>, <span onclick="new Audio('../phonemes/fils.mp3').play()" style="cursor:pointer;"><strong>Fils</strong></span>
    + <span onclick="new Audio('../phonemes/et.mp3').play()" style="cursor:pointer;"><strong>et</strong></span> ("and") -- the t is always silent

French spelling often keeps historical letters that speech has dropped. Sounding every written final consonant produces English-flavored French. The main place those silent finals return is liaison.

## Liaison

When a word ends in a consonant letter that is silent in isolation, and the next word begins with a vowel or mute h, that consonant is often *pronounced and linked* to the following word. This is liaison. It is ordinary standard French, required in many fixed phrases, and normal in careful prayer.

Without it, the prayers sound choppy and letter-by-letter. With it, <span tooltip="Hail Mary">Je vous salue, Marie</span> and <span tooltip="Our Father">Notre Père</span> run as continuous lines.

!!! success "Rule"
    1. Word ends in a normally silent final consonant letter (s, t, x, d, n, …)  
    2. Next word begins with a vowel or mute h  
    3. The consonant is sounded and begins the next syllable  

    Common sound values in liaison:  
    + *s / x / z* → <span onclick="new Audio('../phonemes/z.mp3').play()" style="cursor:pointer;"><strong>z</strong></span>  
    + *d* → often *t*  
    + *t* → *t*  

!!! success "In the Rosary"
    + <span onclick="new Audio('../phonemes/est-avec.mp3').play()" style="cursor:pointer;"><strong>est avec</strong></span> -- t of *est* before *avec*  
    + <span onclick="new Audio('../phonemes/aux-cieux.mp3').play()" style="cursor:pointer;"><strong>aux cieux</strong></span> -- x of *aux* as <span onclick="new Audio('../phonemes/z.mp3').play()" style="cursor:pointer;"><strong>z</strong></span> before <span onclick="new Audio('../phonemes/cieux.mp3').play()" style="cursor:pointer;"><strong>cieux</strong></span>  
    + <span onclick="new Audio('../phonemes/vous-etes.mp3').play()" style="cursor:pointer;"><strong>vous êtes</strong></span> -- s of <span onclick="new Audio('../phonemes/vous.mp3').play()" style="cursor:pointer;"><strong>vous</strong></span> as <span onclick="new Audio('../phonemes/z.mp3').play()" style="cursor:pointer;"><strong>z</strong></span> before *êtes*  

    Strongly expected after short function words such as *les, des, aux, ces, mon, ton, son, mes, tes, ses, nos, vos, un, une, est, sont, nous, vous, ils, elles, très, tout*.

    Never make liaison on the t of <span onclick="new Audio('../phonemes/et.mp3').play()" style="cursor:pointer;"><strong>et</strong></span>.

*Mute h* allows liaison and elision -- e.g. <span onclick="new Audio('../phonemes/lhomme.mp3').play()" style="cursor:pointer;"><strong>l'homme</strong></span>, <span onclick="new Audio('../phonemes/les-hommes.mp3').play()" style="cursor:pointer;"><strong>les hommes</strong></span>. *Aspirated h* blocks them -- e.g. <span onclick="new Audio('../phonemes/le-heros.mp3').play()" style="cursor:pointer;"><strong>le héros</strong></span> -- even though no h sound is heard. The Rosary texts mainly use mute-h patterns.

*Enchaînement* is different: a final consonant that is already pronounced simply continues into a following vowel -- <span onclick="new Audio('../phonemes/notre.mp3').play()" style="cursor:pointer;"><strong>Notre</strong></span> into <span onclick="new Audio('../phonemes/pere.mp3').play()" style="cursor:pointer;"><strong>Père</strong></span>. No silent letter returns; the chain was already audible.

*Mute e* (<span onclick="new Audio('../phonemes/mute-e.mp3').play()" style="cursor:pointer;"><strong>e</strong></span>) in phrases: careful Rosary recitation often keeps a light schwa in words such as <span onclick="new Audio('../phonemes/notre.mp3').play()" style="cursor:pointer;"><strong>Notre</strong></span>, which supports the rhythm. Casual speech drops more of these. Prefer the clearer, moderately paced audio on the prayer pages.

## Rhythm

French does not use English-style word stress that changes meaning (e.g., in English a *REcord* is a noun, and *reCORD* is a verb). 
In French, prominence falls on the *last full syllable of a phrase group*. Pitch often rises for continuation and falls at the end of a statement. 
Syllables stay clear and even; that steadiness is much of what makes French sound so beautiful. 
Put English stress habits on French -- punch the first syllable, swallow the rest -- and the same beautiful words turn to mush.

+ <span onclick="new Audio('../phonemes/marie.mp3').play()" style="cursor:pointer;"><strong>ma-RIE</strong></span>
+ <span onclick="new Audio('../phonemes/sanctifie.mp3').play()" style="cursor:pointer;"><strong>sanc-ti-FIÉ</strong></span>
+ <span onclick="new Audio('../phonemes/pere.mp3').play()" style="cursor:pointer;"><strong>PÈRE</strong></span>

When praying, group by sense: *Au nom du Père // et du Fils // et du Saint-Esprit*.
Each group carries a light final accent. 
That even pacing is normal French prayer speech.

Listen carefully to the steady rhythm of the French prayers.

## <span tooltip="Recommendations">Recommandations</span>

Clear metropolitan French Rosary recordings are the best models for liaison, <span onclick="new Audio('../phonemes/mute-e.mp3').play()" style="cursor:pointer;"><strong>mute e</strong></span>, and phrase rhythm. Listen to full phrases, then speak with them.

[Phonétique progressive du français](https://www.cle-international.com/recherche?controller=search&s=phon%C3%A9tique+progressive) (Clé International) covers vowels, nasals, and liaison in a graded series.

[Michel Thomas French](https://www.michelthomas.com/) trains pure vowels, <span onclick="new Audio('../phonemes/u.mp3').play()" style="cursor:pointer;"><strong>u</strong></span> versus <span onclick="new Audio('../phonemes/ou.mp3').play()" style="cursor:pointer;"><strong>ou</strong></span>, and uvular <span onclick="new Audio('../phonemes/r.mp3').play()" style="cursor:pointer;"><strong>r</strong></span> through oral imitation.

[Lawless French -- Pronunciation](https://www.lawlessfrench.com/pronunciation/) collects spelling-sound maps and liaison tables for English speakers.

[Forvo](https://forvo.com/languages/fr/) for single-word native checks -- e.g. <span onclick="new Audio('../phonemes/grace.mp3').play()" style="cursor:pointer;"><strong>grâce</strong></span>, <span onclick="new Audio('../phonemes/regne.mp3').play()" style="cursor:pointer;"><strong>règne</strong></span>, <span onclick="new Audio('../phonemes/seigneur.mp3').play()" style="cursor:pointer;"><strong>Seigneur</strong></span>.

Each prayer page includes audio for every passage.

## <span tooltip="Not Recommended">Non Recommandé</span>

Reading French with English letter values -- sounding every final consonant, turning <span onclick="new Audio('../phonemes/u.mp3').play()" style="cursor:pointer;"><strong>u</strong></span> into English "oo", or using an English or Spanish <span onclick="new Audio('../phonemes/r.mp3').play()" style="cursor:pointer;"><strong>r</strong></span>. That habit blocks liaison and the prayer line.

Methods that promise quick fluency while skipping phonetics. Nasals, front rounded <span onclick="new Audio('../phonemes/u.mp3').play()" style="cursor:pointer;"><strong>u</strong></span>, uvular <span onclick="new Audio('../phonemes/r.mp3').play()" style="cursor:pointer;"><strong>r</strong></span>, and liaison need clear models. See [bad advice](../../english/resources/bad-advice.md).

Street reductions that drop every <span onclick="new Audio('../phonemes/mute-e.mp3').play()" style="cursor:pointer;"><strong>mute e</strong></span> and required liaison are poor models for the Rosary. Prefer clear, moderately paced parish or monastic French, where phrases stay intelligible and accents stay audible.
