# <span tooltip="Pronunciation">Prononciation</span>

French descends directly from the Vulgar Latin spoken in Roman Gaul. After the collapse of the Western Empire, this spoken Latin developed over centuries into the language we call French. The oldest surviving text in a form of French is the Serment de Strasbourg of 842. Old French appears in the great medieval epics, and by the time of the Ordinance of Villers-Cotterêts in 1539 French had replaced Latin as the language of law and royal administration in France.

While the official liturgy of the Church remained in Latin for most of its history, the Rosary has always been a prayer of the people. In French cathedrals, villages, and later in Quebec and across the missions, the faithful recited "Notre Père" and "Je vous salue, Marie" in their own tongue. The Académie française, founded in 1635, worked to fix spelling and pronunciation standards that still guide the clear, traditional delivery of these prayers today. The result is a measured, flowing style perfectly suited to the steady repetition of the Rosary.

## Phonemes

French pronunciation rests on a system of pure vowels, four nasal vowels, the uvular r, and the crucial rule of liaison. The written accents are not decorative; they mark precise vowel qualities required for correct prayer.

!!! success "Nasal Vowels"
    Nasal vowels are produced by lowering the soft palate so air escapes through both nose and mouth. They are essential to the sound of French prayer.

    + <span onclick="new Audio('../phonemes/an.mp3').play()" style="cursor:pointer;"><strong>an / en / am / em</strong></span> "ahn" nasal
    + <span onclick="new Audio('../phonemes/in.mp3').play()" style="cursor:pointer;"><strong>in / im / ain</strong></span> "an" nasal
    + <span onclick="new Audio('../phonemes/on.mp3').play()" style="cursor:pointer;"><strong>on / om</strong></span> "ohn" nasal
    + <span onclick="new Audio('../phonemes/un.mp3').play()" style="cursor:pointer;"><strong>un / um</strong></span> "uhn" nasal

!!! note "Accents and Diacritics"
    The five French accents control vowel quality and sometimes word identity. They must be respected when reading or praying.

    + é (accent aigu) = closed "ay" as in "say" -- sanctifié, béni, Jésus
    + è (accent grave) = open "eh" as in "bed" -- Père, règne, Mère
    + ê (accent circonflexe) = usually open "eh"; often marks a lost historical "s" -- fête (from old feste)
    + ç (cédille) = soft "s" before a, o, u -- français
    + à, ù = same sounds as a and u but used to distinguish words (à = "to")

    In the Rosary, Père always carries the grave accent on the open e. Sanctifié carries the acute on the final closed e. These marks are audible.

!!! success "Consonants and Key Features"
    + <span onclick="new Audio('../phonemes/r.mp3').play()" style="cursor:pointer;"><strong>r</strong></span> uvular -- light vibration or gargle at the back of the throat
    + <span onclick="new Audio('../phonemes/pere.mp3').play()" style="cursor:pointer;"><strong>Père</strong></span> example with r and grave accent
    + <span onclick="new Audio('../phonemes/est-avec.mp3').play()" style="cursor:pointer;"><strong>est avec</strong></span> -- final consonant pronounced and linked when next word begins with vowel or silent h (liaison)
    + <span onclick="new Audio('../phonemes/aux-cieux.mp3').play()" style="cursor:pointer;"><strong>aux cieux</strong></span> example of liaison
    + <span onclick="new Audio('../phonemes/ch.mp3').play()" style="cursor:pointer;"><strong>ch</strong></span> = "sh"
    + <span onclick="new Audio('../phonemes/j.mp3').play()" style="cursor:pointer;"><strong>j</strong></span> = "zh" (as in "pleasure")
    + gn = "ny"
    + final consonants are normally silent unless liaison applies

Liaison is not optional decoration in prayer. It creates the continuous, chanting line that makes the French Ave Maria and Pater Noster flow so naturally.

    The repeated structure and nasal sounds in "Saint-Esprit" give this doxology its characteristic gentle rhythm.

## <span tooltip="Recommendations">Recommandations</span>

Listen repeatedly to clear recordings of the Rosary prayers in French. Imitate the pronunciation, rhythm, and flow.

Traditional monastic and parish recordings from French-speaking communities provide excellent models for the measured delivery used in prayer.

Speak the words aloud while following a recording. Begin with the Sign of the Cross, Our Father, Hail Mary, and Glory Be. Focus especially on the nasal vowels, uvular r, liaisons, and accented syllables such as those in Père and sanctifié.

Once the core prayers are comfortable, continue with the remaining prayers.

---

## <span tooltip="Not Recommended">Non Recommandé</span>

Do not rely on generic language apps or "speak like a native in 30 days" methods for the specific demands of prayer pronunciation. The nasals, uvular r, and rule-governed liaisons require attentive imitation of good models, not shortcuts. See [bad advice](../../english/resources/bad-advice.md).
